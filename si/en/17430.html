There are two compulsory field trips in the program.

The first is the mandatory field course, which takes place during the first year. This course includes a field stay of two to three weeks, typically in a village in East Africa or in South or Southeast Asia, though the location may vary. During the course, you will collect primary data on real-world problems related to Environment and Development and collaborate with students from local universities. You can expect a steep learning curve and an experience for life. The university provides financial support for this field trip.

The second compulsory component is the field stay related to your thesis. You are required to spend a minimum of two months collecting data. The possibilities for thesis fieldwork are wide-ranging. You might conduct fieldwork in a pastoralist community in Kenya, work from an air-conditioned office at the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) in Rome, travel on horseback to experimental fields in the mountains of Lesotho, or interview mushroom collectors in Poland. Many other options are possible, provided that the work is relevant to the Environment and Development program.

As noted above under “Thesis", the university sometimes provides financial support for thesis-related fieldwork conducted abroad. Be sure to familiarize yourself with the specific conditions under which this support is available. With some initiative, it is often also possible to secure additional funding from external sources.

Fieldwork is a strict requirement in the Environment and Development program. This is because many essential skills and insights cannot be acquired in a classroom setting, and future employers are likely to value and actively seek out graduates with substantial fieldwork experience.
