What should my CV contain?

When creating a CV, the most important thing is that you

  • include the sections and elements that best describe you and your relevant experiences
  • always tailor and target the content to the specific position you are applying for.

This means that you need to adjust your CV every time you apply for a job.

Typical elements in a CV

NB: Always list your experiences from education and work with the newest and most current first.

Profile


A brief introductory text (max 5-8 lines), that should

  • catch the employer's attention
  • show how your profile specifically fits the job and the company.

Highlight only the skills and motivation that are relevant to the current job. You can create a bullet point list of the skills you particularly want to highlight.

Give your profile text a meaningful title that reflects the skills and profile needed for the current position.

Photo

Unless stated otherwise in the job advertisement, it's a good idea to have a photo in your CV.

Use a portrait photo where you

  • look like yourself
  • are in front of a neutral background
  • appear professional – preferably with a friendly smile.
Education

Work experience typically comes first after your profile text. However, if you are a student or recent graduate with little work experience, you can start with your education.

  • Write the titles of your education and the years indicating when you started and completed them.
  • Select and highlight the courses and projects that are relevant to the current job.
  • Under each course/project, write down the skills you have gained from them – e.g. which methods you master.
  • If it makes sense for the position, you can highlight high grades (10 or 12). In PhD positions, for example, this often matters. Outside the academic world, it typically doesn't have the same significance.
Work experience (including student jobs)

Write the position, company, and period of employment for each position.

Describe your tasks and responsibilities – selected based on the position you are applying for.

Also, write down the skills you have gained through your various tasks, showing why it is relevant to the job you are seeking.

Volunteer experience

Write the position, company, and the period you have been volunteering.

Describe your tasks and responsibilities.

Highlight the skills you have gained through your voluntary work, showing why it is relevant to the application.

IT and languages

Write down your IT and language skills, and what level they are at, for example:

"Super user" or "proficient user"

"Fluent", “proficient" or "intermediate".

Publications


List publications of articles you have written during your education – but only if they are relevant to the current job.

Personal info

Write about 5 lines about who you are and what you do and are interested in outside of your studies or work.

This gives the employer an impression of who you are as a person.

Name and contact details

Write your name and contact details at the beginning of the CV, so it is easy for the employer to find. Also, consider linking to your LinkedIn profile.

Do not write your CPR number on the CV.

References

Write in a section at the end that the employer can get your references* upon request. This way, you have the opportunity to

  • prepare your references for a call
  • select who the employer should contact.

Do not write your references directly on the CV.

*Former employers, teachers, or supervisors who are willing to share their impression of you with a potential employer.
