My advice to you on how to be a successful student in the Global Environment and Development programme:

Build a network

When you enter the MSc in Global Environment and Development programme, you access a network of professionals with different cultural and academic backgrounds, including the lecturers at the university. This is your future network; make sure you get the most out of it by engaging yourself academically and socially at the university.

The Global Environment and Development students traditionally spend much time together on courses – including through the fieldwork experience in block 3 – providing you with a solid foundation for building your network.

Keep an eye open for contacts who may be able to inspire you, support you, or help you get in touch with your favourite organisation or the company that can host your next project. Or perhaps they just have contacts in the country where you are going for thesis fieldwork, which can help you move on.

Also, note that you will have the opportunity during the first year to meet faculty members from your second-year university – they will be visiting Copenhagen and will take part in some of the first-year teachings.


Learn to navigate the Danish study culture

Studying at a Danish university may differ from what you are used to. Specifically, there is an open and relaxed atmosphere between students and professors. This is an opportunity to engage and benefit from discussions and contacts – rather than becoming frustrated by having to take responsibility for your own study progress. You may find the “discussion culture" annoying if you expect more top-down teaching – use the opportunity to engage.

I strongly recommend you take a strategic approach to your courses. Ask explicitly for performance criteria: What is a good assignment? Can we see one? Which criteria are used for exam evaluation? You have a right to know these things, which may differ from what you expect.

Professors are ready to help you, but we often see that it takes a few courses before international students find the study model that works for them, and that is too long - engage with lecturers from the start to avoid frustrated expectations.


Evaluate your courses

Professors welcome input on how to improve courses. In Copenhagen, all courses are student evaluated; use this opportunity to express and share your ideas about how we can improve courses. I will not promise to follow your advice, but we will consider it! Written evaluations are anonymous.


Never plagiarise - learn the principles of academic integrity

You must never plagiarise in your assignments. I know you will never do this, but tell your friends too!

We run routine checks on all assignments, and cheating will be detected. And we are harsh in our responses. I believe we must be to protect the integrity and value of our degrees.

I mention this explicitly as I do not want to find myself in the position of having to expel a student for plagiarism. Please make sure this does not happen.

Again, the professors will happily explain what is okay and what is not, so there is no reason to start guessing.


Form a study group

Studying in a master's programme can be tough. There is a lot of work, the others look more focused or organised, and they are already planning their thesis in the second year. I strongly recommend you team up in study groups (in many courses, you will have no choice) and help each other – with the coursework and life in general. It is all part of networking, and you will soon realise that the others also have doubts about their choice of courses, etc.

Take part in meetings and social activities

There will be various meetings for the GED group, where we discuss the choice of courses, meet new people, etc. Besides, numerous events are organised by staff, students, or interest groups at or around the university. Take advantage of these opportunities while you are here.
