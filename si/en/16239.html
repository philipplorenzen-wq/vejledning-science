Please note: This chapter is for students following the specialization "Chemistry, Toxicology and Health" and "Soil and Water" only. Students following the specialization "Soil, water and biodiversity (EnvEuro)" should look here for their programme structure and study options.

For the specialization in “Chemistry, Toxicology and Health" the first two blocks consists of compulsory courses and you have one compulsory course in each of block 3 and 4, whereas the specialization in “Soil and Water" have one compulsory course in each of block 1 and 3. For the remaining blocks and half blocks, you have to choose courses which will support your scientific profile and give you the competences needed for your Master's thesis . Before every block there are selection deadline. Therefore, if you need to discuss course choices with me or with representatives from the different scientific fields, then remember to do it in due time before the deadlines.

MSc thesis examples

Your thesis can be about many different environmental issues. In the list below you can read the descriptions of 7 very different theses, authored by students at the MSc Programme in Environmental Science. You are welcome to contact the author if you have further questions regarding a specific thesis.

Effects of Carbon Nanotubes on the Female Reproductive Axis: Interference with the female reproductive cycle. A Study in Mice.

 

Author: Hanna Lilith Katarina Johansson

Supervisor: Karin Sørig Hougaard; National Research Centre for the Working Environment & University of Copenhagen

Very little is known on the effects of nanoparticles on reproductive function. Inhalation of nanoparticles induces inflammation in the lung – and inflammation interferes with the female reproductive axis. In this study, we investigated the effect of airway exposure to multi walled CNTs (MWCNTs) on the estrous cycle of female mice. The results showed that female reproduction may be susceptible to acute exposure to nanoparticles.

Degradation of tetrachloroethylene using green rust-bone char composite

Author: Titus Carlsson 

Supervisors: Hans Christian Bruun Hansen and Jing Ai, Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, University of Copenhagen

A newly discovered iron hydroxide (green rust) - bone char composite has been proven to efficiently degrade chlorinated ethylenes in batch studies. In this study, column experiments were performed to analyse the reductive properties and behaviour of the composite material in a dynamic setup. PCE-spiked groundwater was pumped through columns packed with a sand-composite mixture. The results showed a decrease of PCE concentrations in the pore water as it passes through the column, while also showing the production of acetylene and the probable intermediate propyne. Sampling during stopped-flow periods inside the columns shows that the composite is reacting and is capable of fully removing PCE from the groundwater. The combination of strong adsorption and degradation makes the green rust- bone char composite a viable material for use in situ chemical reduction of chlorinated ethylenes.


Structure-activity analysis of new pollution control materials

Author: Szymon Kwiatkowski

Supervisors: Matthew S. Johnson (msj@chem.ku.dk), Department of Chemistry, University of Copenhagen; Hasse C. Knap, Airlabs

Ambient air pollution, particularly in urban areas, often exceeds the levels which are recognized as safe for human health. Furthermore, the air quality of indoor environments, where people spend the majority of their time is also of great concern. Awareness of the health impacts of air pollution is growing rapidly and has resulted in the increasing popularity of pollution control devices (PCD). The development of PCD's emphasizes the need for novel materials for pollution control. Hence, graphitic materials, such as graphene oxide (GO) are recently gaining increasing attention. The focus of this thesis lies in the investigation of graphene oxide (GO) and its 3-D structures as a promising, new material for air pollution removal. The study explores graphene's  physicochemical properties under exposure to 3 common ambient and indoor pollutants: nitrogen dioxide, sulfur dioxide, and formaldehyde.

Aquatic toxicity of plant alkaloids in Daphnia magna

Author: Megan Rose Griffiths

Supervisors: Bjarne W. Strobel (bjwe@plen.ku.dk) and Nina Cedergreen (ncf@plen.ku.dk), Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, University of Copenhagen.

Plants produce secondary metabolites that may be toxic to organisms in the environment. Alkaloids are among these metabolites and many are also quite persistent. Change of cropping systems for example to use lupin as a protein crop may increase the amount of lupin derived alkaloids in neighboring aquatic environments. In this thesis both acute and chronic toxicity of lupin alkaloids were tested, and alkaloid mixtures were compared to the toxicity of the lupin plant extracts. Alkaloids did show toxic effects towards daphnids, and comparing toxicity with measured alkaloid concentrations defined a potential risk in high exposure stream environments.

Modelling of natural toxin fate in soil – a case of ptaquiloside for Denmark

Author: Daniel Garcia Jørgensen 

Supervisor: Hans Christian Bruun Hansen (haha@plen.ku.dk), Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, University of Copenhagen.

Bracken fern is widely distributed throughout the world. Bracken is carcinogenic when ingested by animals or humans. The carcinogenicity is related to several toxins, of which the most important is ptaquiloside (PTA). PTA is highly soluble and therefore prone to leaching. The aim of this study was to study the environmental fate of PTA using Daisy, a soil-water-plant-atmosphere model with special focus on release of PTA from fronds during precipitation events. The model predicted realistic concentrations of PTA in the soil and in the leachate with maximum concentrations of 60 and 10 μg l-1for the two soils studied. Future  climate scenarios for Denmark were assessed in the model. With predicted higher precipitations and temperatures in the future, it is expected a longer growing season for bracken and higher PTA leaching. 


Linking crude oil biodegradation in temperate and arctic marine environments to associated water phase toxicity

 

Author: Mette Kristensen

Supervisor: Jan H. Christensen (jch@plen.ku.dk); Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, Faculty of Science, University of Copenhagen.

In this interdisciplinary master thesis, degradation of crude oil hydrocarbons incubated in sea water from either pristine Greenlandic waters or from heavily trafficked North Sea water was investigated and the toxicity of the degradation products tested. The hydrocarbons were measured by SPE - GC-MS and linked to changes in microbial and algal toxicity (assessed by Microtox Assay, Leucine incorporation, MPN enumeration and algae growth inhibition test). The degradation order and toxicity pattern showed distinct differences between the temperate and arctic marine environments and changed significantly over time.


Remediation of chromated copper arsenate (CCA) contaminated sites with biochar and zero-valent iron

Author: Hanna Frick

Supervisors: Kristian Koefoed Brandt (kkb@plen.ku.dk), Ellen Kandeler, Peter Engelund Holm

Chromated copper arsenates (CCA) have been widely used for wood impregnation and are a major source of metal pollution in soil. Being highly toxic and carcinogenic, CCAs threaten both human and environmental health, including groundwater due to possible leaching. In-situ stabilization by application of immobilizing amendments is one approach by which bioavailability and mobility of the metal(loid)s can be reduced. A combination of zero-valent iron (ZVI) and biochar (BC), has been sugested as promising amendment for metal immobilization. In a microcosm experiment, soil samples from a former CCA contaminated wood impregnation site, were treated with ZVI + BC, and incubated for 56 days. The combined treatment with ZVI and fine BC was the most efficient one and showed a significant effect in reducing water-extractable and bioavailable Cu and As; water-extractable Cr was strongly reduced with increasing bacterial growth rate. Biosensor analysis revealed that the water-extractable fraction generally reflected the bioavailable metal fraction, however, biochar only reduced bioavailable but not water-extractable Cu.
