What kinds of competences do I have, and which can I build up?

Throughout your life, you develop both

  1. academic competences related to your degree programme, student jobs, internships etc.
  2. personal competences that are related to your personality and affect how you approach tasks and work with others.

Academic competences you gain through your studies

When you study for a degree or work in a certain subject area, you develop specific competences related to the fields in question.

For you as a student, the requirements for your academic competences are already formulated for you in:

  • Section 5 of the competency description of your programme-specific curriculum. You can find it here at Study Information under Curricula and rules
  • The descriptions of the learning goals in your course descriptions. You can find them by searching for your courses on www.kurser.ku.dk.

You can call something you have learned a competence when you are able to apply it yourself in practice – for example a method of analysis or a lab technique.


General academic compentences

In the course of your studies, you will also build up some general academic competences. General academic competences include the ability to:

  • grasp and delimit a problem in a systematic way
  • analyse data, deal with large amounts of information and communicate it  
  • quickly acquire new knowledge and conceptual frameworks.

Academic competences you gain from student jobs, internships etc.

In addition to your study programme, you may have gained relevant competences through student jobs, internships, political or volunteer work etc.
Examples of academic competences gained from student jobs, internships etc. are:

  • Administrative competences – for example, you can work systematically and maintain an overview of detailed administrative tasks.
  • Communication competences – for example, you can communicate content precisely in speech and/or writing, update websites etc.
  • Organisational or business competences – for example, you understand how a particular type of organisation or company works, and how your role and tasks contribute.
  • Intercultural competences – for example, you have experience of working with people from different cultures, understand how to communicate with them and how you can help each other.

Personal competences

Your personal competences are related to your personality and are expressed in how you approach tasks and work with others.

For example, you may be good at

  • working with others and getting everyone in the group to contribute
  • making decisions
  • staying calm in stressful situations
  • giving and receiving feedback.

You may want to ask fellow students you have worked with during your studies what they think your personal competences are. It can be hard to describe one's own qualities and skills.
