Sustainability is at the very heart of what you will be studying. As an ENRE student, you will develop a deep understanding of how economic tools and thinking can be applied to some of the most pressing environmental and sustainability challenges of our time, from climate change and biodiversity loss to natural resource management and green policy design. Compulsory courses give you the foundation and as you move into your second year, you can tailor your studies towards the sustainability themes that interest you most.

Here are three sustainability-focused study profiles based on the ENRE structure, each built around a clear theme. They are illustrative, not prescriptions, but each represents a coherent and recognizable area of expertise within environmental and resource economics.

Profile 1: Climate and Environmental Policy For students interested in designing and evaluating climate and environmental policy in ministries, EU institutions, NGOs, or think tanks. This profile prepares you to analyze, design and evaluate environmental and climate policy, working with the economic instruments that governments use to address market failures such as pollution and biodiversity loss.

  • Compulsory courses will give you a strong foundation: Incentives and Regulation covers the theory of carbon taxes, emissions trading, and other policy instruments, while Economic Valuation Methods and Cost-Benefit Analysis teaches you how to assess whether policies are worth their cost. Natural Resource Economics adds the long-run resource perspective essential for climate analysis.
  • You could choose your restricted elective course to deepen this. Impact Evaluation (Block 4, year 1) would give you the causal inference methods, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, synthetic control, instruments that are used by government agencies and the European Commission to assess whether policies work. Other natural course if you are more policy oriented could be Working as a Consultant (Block 4, year 1) simulating real consulting briefs of the kind you would encounter at COWI, Rambøll, or the Danish Environmental Protection Agency developing structured problem decomposition, rapid evidence synthesis, concise analytical writing, and high-stakes presentation skills or Agricultural and Food Policy (Block 4, year 1) that covers climate policy, payments for ecosystem services, deforestation, land use, and food security through the lens of the EU Common Agricultural Policy and global agri-food instruments. Applied Economics of Forest & Nature (Block 2, year 2) would extend your understanding of biodiversity and ecosystem service valuation, payments for ecosystem services, and the economics of the EU Nature Restoration Law. Advanced Development Economics (Block 2, year 2) would add the international dimension, covering how environmental policies interact with development in low- and middle-income countries.
  • You could choose your two free electives to add a complementary strand for example EU environmental governance, environmental law, or a quantitative methods course or focusing more on for example conservation biology.

Profile 2: Sustainable Consumption and Food Systems For students interested in how households, firms, and food systems can transition towards sustainability in consumer policy, food industry, sustainability consultancy, or research on dietary change.

  • The demand side of sustainability is often overlooked, but household choices about food, energy, and consumption drive a significant share of environmental impact. The compulsory courses in Economic Valuation Methods and Cost-Benefit Analysis and Incentives and Regulation give you the tools to understand both what sustainability is worth to consumers and how policy can shift behaviour.
  • You could choose the following restricted elective courses. Applied Economics of Consumption (Block 4, year1) would be the core of this profile covering demand systems, discrete choice models, eco-labelling, and the sustainability transition in consumption patterns. Behavioural and Experimental Economics (Block 1, year 2) complements this directly, providing the theory and methods behind nudge policies, social norms, and the design of behavioural interventions for pro-environmental and dietary change.  Applied Economics of Forest & Nature (Block 2, year 2) adds the supply-side perspective on sustainable land use and the nature-food system nexus. For the more business oriented natural choices could be Industrial Organization (block 1, year 2) or Economic Efficiency and Benchmarking (block 2, year 2)
  • As your 15 ECTS free elective, consider Entrepreneurship and Innovation to explore the business model dimension of sustainable food systems, or Business Development and Innovation if you are aiming for a career in the private sector. A course in nutrition policy or food law from another UCPH faculty can also be a distinctive addition.

Profile 3: Global Environment and Sustainable Development For students interested in environmental challenges in a global and development context, in international organisations (World Bank, FAO, CGIAR, DANIDA), global NGOs, or international environmental governance.

  • The compulsory curriculum provides strong foundations: Natural Resource Economics gives you the formal theory of renewable resource management, fisheries, forests, water, which is all resources under acute pressure in developing contexts; Incentives & Regulation covers the design of environmental policy instruments including international environmental agreements; and Economic Valuation Methods & CBA gives you the tools to appraise environmental policies and projects from a societal perspective.
  • You could choose your restricted elective courses as follows: Advanced Development Economics (block 2, year 2) would be the cornerstone of this profile, covering household and firm behaviour under resource constraints, market failures in developing contexts, credit constraints, information asymmetries, missing markets and the microeconomics of deforestation, water access and climate adaptation. Advanced International Trade (Block 1, year 2) would add the trade-environment nexus: carbon leakage, the pollution haven hypothesis, carbon border adjustment mechanisms and the WTO compatibility of environmental policies. Impact Evaluation (block 4, year 1) would provide the causal inference methods required by international organizations to evaluate projects, but for the more policy interested student Agricultural & Food Policy might be a better choice. Opportunities Outside the Classroom

  • Sustainability does not stop at the lecture hall. The Project in Practice (PIP) is one of the most valuable ways to engage with real-world sustainability challenges, a 15 ECTS supervised project conducted in partnership with an external organisation such as a government ministry, NGO, research institute, or company. It is the program's most hands-on learning experience and is highly valued by employers.
  • Your thesis topics are your chance to tackle genuine sustainability challenges, whether in climate policy, resource management, biodiversity, or sustainable development.
  • Getting involved in student organisations, attending conferences on green transition and environmental economics, and seeking out internships with public institutions or sustainability-focused firms are all excellent ways to complement your academic studies and build a meaningful profile in the field
