Try out these 2 methods developed by your study- and career guidance counsellors. Begin with method 1.


Method #1 Identifying your competences

To identify and describe your competences, it is a good idea to start with some specific examples of situations where you have used them.

This exercise will help you get a general overview of your personal competences and things you have learned and done in your life so far that may be relevant to your career. 

How it works:

  1. Open the form here and save it on your computer – if necessary, adapt it to suit your needs. identify your skills.docx
  2. Fill in the form based on your courses and projects, student job, volunteer work and leisure interests.

  3. For each line you fill in, answer the following questions under these headings
    - Work task: Briefly describe the specific task. What were you asked to do
    - Result/success story: What was the outcome? Are there any specific results or success stories you can highlight?
    - Competences: What competences did you use? Think through how you solved the task.

Next step

Now you can use your completed form to

  • identify which competences are your particular strengths, which you have most experience with using and which you prefer to use
  • describe specific examples when you introduce yourself and your competences, e.g. in a CV, job application or job interview.

Save the form and expand it when you learn new things.

 

Method #2 Competence spotting

It can be hard to put into words what you are actually good at. So it might be a good idea to ask your friends, fellow students or colleagues for help.
This exercise asks you to get help from your network to identify your competences and practise describing your competences using specific examples from your student and working life.


How it works:

  1. Find someone who wants to help you.
  2. Make sure to have pen and paper ready so you can both take notes.
  3. Your role: Describe a specific situation where you made a difference or solved a task. This can be in any context, in your studies or personal life. Talk to your partner about this for about 10 minutes.
    Your partner's role: Listen, ask questions and write down all the competences you used in the situations you describe.
  4. When you have both finished describing, listening and writing down, your partner reads aloud all the competences they spotted.
  5. End the exercise by discussing whether there are other competences you can add to the list.


Questions to help you tell your story:

  • What was the task about?
  • What role did you play in it?
  • How did you handle the task?
  • What happened along the way?
  • Who did you work with, and how did it go?
  • How did you solve the task?
  • What was the outcome?

Next step

When you have finished the exercise, you might want to consider

  • repeating the exercise with different situations and other people (with new perspectives)
  • describing the same situations to other people and see if they spot the same competences in you, or different ones.

Make sure to update your form from method #1 with the new competences you have discovered.
