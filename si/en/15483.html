Below, I outline a few suggestions for how you can make the most of the Environment and Development program.

Build a strong network

When you begin studying, you enter a professional community that includes fellow students and lecturers from many cultural and academic backgrounds. This network is an invaluable resource. Make the most of it by engaging in it, academically and socially.

Throughout the program, you will spend significant time with other students, particularly during courses and during fieldwork in Block 3. These settings offer excellent opportunities to expand your network.

Keep an eye out for people who can inspire you, support your academic development, and help you connect with organizations and companies where you may want to undertake your next project. Some of them could have contacts in the country where you plan to conduct fieldwork. Building these relationships can benefit you not only during your studies but also in the long term.

Learn to Navigate the Danish Study Culture

Studying at a Danish university may differ from the academic environments you are used to. Many international students notice a relatively informal and open atmosphere between students and professors. It is important to understand that this also implies that faculty members generally expect you to take responsibility for your own learning and to be proactive in your studies. The Danish “discussion culture" can feel unfamiliar or even frustrating if you are accustomed to more directive or top‑down teaching. However, you may also see it as an opportunity to engage actively and benefit from dialogue and collaboration.

I strongly recommend approaching your classes strategically. Ask lecturers and students about the criteria for successful assignments and request examples when possible. Familiarize yourself with the assessment requirements for each exam. You are entitled to this information, and it may differ from what you expect. All over, engage with your professors and lecturers from the very beginning. As a rule, all faculty members are willing to support and to help you figure out which study strategies work best for you. 

Evaluate your courses

At the University of Copenhagen, students evaluate all courses. We encourage you to make use of this opportunity to share your thoughts and suggestions for improvement. Faculty members genuinely appreciate constructive feedback, and there is a formal procedure in place to ensure that your input is considered when planning the following year's course. While I cannot guarantee that all suggestions will be implemented, I can assure you they will always be considered. Official written evaluations are completely anonymous.

Read more about course evaluations.

Never Plagiarise - Learn the Principles of Academic Integrity

While I trust that you will not plagiarize, I also encourage you to remind your peers to uphold these standards. All assignments are routinely screened for plagiarism, and any form of cheating is taken very seriously. These measures exist to protect the integrity and value of the university's degrees.

I emphasize this because it is deeply unfortunate when a student must be sanctioned, or even expelled, for plagiarism. Please ensure that this does not become an issue. If you are unsure about proper citation practices or what constitutes acceptable academic conduct, the Faculty is available to clarify expectations so that nothing is left to guesswork.

Learn more about the university's rules on plagiarism and academic misconduct.

Form a study group

Beginning a graduate program can be demanding. The workload is substantial, and it is common to feel that others are more focused, more skilled, or further along in planning their fieldwork. I strongly encourage you to form study groups. In many courses they may even be essential. Study groups offer valuable academic support, help you stay organized, and foster a sense of community. They also serve as an important form of networking, and you will soon discover that your peers share many of the same questions, doubts, and concerns that you do.


Take Part in Meetings and Social Activities

From time to time, students or I will organize informal café meetings for the ED group, where we discuss thesis-planning, course selection, career opportunities, and related topics. In addition, staff members, students, and various interest groups host a wide range of events on and around campus. Participating in these activities, whether as an attendee or an organizer, can be highly beneficial, even when your schedule feels tight. The long‑term payoff comes in the form of stronger networks, valuable insights, and renewed energy for your studies.
