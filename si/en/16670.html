You should seek help to handle your stress if: 

  • your physical and psychological signs of stress persist for a long period, or simply get worse and worse 
  • you cannot identify a particular external cause of your stress 
  • you are unsure whether you are OK – for example, if you experience something that is atypical for you. 

Common signs of stress 

Most people have no doubt experienced one or more typical signs of stress, such as: 

  • physical agitation and heart palpitations 
  • irritability 
  • stomach problems 
  • racing thoughts 
  • difficulties falling asleep. 

It is unpleasant – but harmless – to experience common signs of stress for a limited period of time, e.g. up to an exam or an important submission or presentation, or during a particularly busy week. But it is not healthy if you feel uneasy, experience racing thoughts, or have troubled sleep over an extended period. 

There are various ways of handling your unease about exams or a deadline. You could try the exercises on this page under “How can I dampen my anxiety or reduce my stress symptoms?”. 

Are you extremely nervous about exams? Read more under Handle exam anxiety. 


Know yourself and your typical stress signs 

How we are impacted by being busy and stressed varies greatly from person to person – as does what and how much it takes before we develop signs of stress or become clinically stressed. 

You can get better at assessing what is normal for you by: 

  • being aware of what you typically react to and how 
  • avoiding comparing yourself with others. There is a risk you will ignore your own danger signals if you set your expectations for what you should be able to manage based on other people. 

Once you know what is normal for you, it is easier to know when you should react to your stress signs.
