There are many types of communities, which suit different types of people at different times in their student lives. 

Fortunately, you have the opportunity to try out different things during your studies. 

3 things are key to consider if you are unsure about which communities you want to seek out and cultivate. 


 #1 What type of community are you after? 

Think about the social framework with others you like best: 

  • Do you prefer small gatherings – or do you get energised by being with lots of people?
  • Would you like to build a community with a fairly fixed group of people – or would you like to meet and be inspired by many different people?
  • Should social gatherings be organised around activities or specific tasks – or do you prefer more spontaneous and informal interaction where the social element is central?

#2 What do you particularly want from the community? 

Reflect on what you most want to get out of a community. Is it most important for you to: 

  • cultivate and share a special interest with other people, for example, sport or something academic? 
  • meet new people, also from other study programmes? 
  • strengthen the sense of togetherness with people from your study programme or your immediate study environment? 
  • contribute with something specific in your study environment, e.g. politically or socially? 


#3 Regular and committed or casual communities? 

Think about whether what you currently need most/can involve yourself in is: 

  • a regular community over an extended or limited period, where you build up something over time with others, and where people expect you to participate and contribute actively?
  • more loose and spontaneous communities, where you can be social and get involved when it fits in with your student life? 

Examples of regular and committed communities centred around activities: 

  • Political and social associations that meet regularly 
  • Your study group 
  • Volunteering in the café/bar linked to your degree programme 
  • Becoming a tutor on your degree programme 
  • Joining a football team 

Examples of casual communities with and without regular activities: 

  • The board game or running club on campus 
  • Attending the café or Friday bar 
  • Attending academic events, e.g. lectures or workshops, in your study environment
  • Attending the study council’s open general meeting
