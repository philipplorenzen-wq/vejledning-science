My advice to you on how to be a successful student in the Integrated Food Studies MSc programme:

Build a network

When you enter MSc in Integrated Food Studies (IFS), you will also enter a new network of other students as well professionals with different cultural and academic backgrounds, including the lecturers at the university. Besides their different backgrounds, all share your interest in understanding the food system and perhaps how we as professionals can contribute to changes in the food systems. All these professionals may be part of your future network, so make sure you get the most out of it by engaging yourself academically and socially at the university.

The program in Integrated Food Studies is organized in a way where you most of the time of your first study year will follow mandatory course elements. Therefore, I recommend that you do what you can to meet students from the other food-oriented programmes at the faculty: go to extracurricular activities like meetings or seminars at the faculty. Join the student associations and have a drink with fellow students at A-vej.

Learn more about social and political activities at Science

Learn to navigate the Danish study culture

If you are new to the Danish educational system, you may experience that studying at a Danish university is different from what you are used to. Specifically, there is an open and relaxed atmosphere between students and professors. But you may also find the lax atmosphere and strong reliance on your individual choices and responsibility for study progress frustrating. After all, things are easier when people express very clear expectations. You may find the “discussion culture" annoying if you expect more hard facts – this will depend on yourself and the specific course.

I recommend you take a strategic approach to your courses. Ask explicitly for performance criteria: What is a good assignment? Which criteria are we evaluated by at the exam? You have a right to know these things, and it may be different from what you expect.

Professors are ready to help you, but we often see that it takes a few courses before international students find the model that works, and that is too long - engage with lecturers from the start to avoid frustrated expectations.

Evaluate your courses

Professors welcome input on how to improve courses. At the University of Copenhagen, all courses are evaluated; please use this opportunity to express and share your ideas about how we can improve courses. Remember: if you don't share your experiences – good or bad – with the course responsible, things may never change.

I can't, of course, promise that we follow your advice, but we will promise that all evaluations are considered by the educational committee at the department as well as by the course responsible.

Read more about course evaluations.

Never plagiarize - learn the principles of academic integrity

You must never plagiarize in your assignments. I know you will never do this, but tell your friends as well.

We run routine checks on all assignments and cheating will be detected. And we are quite harsh in our responses. I believe we must be strict to protect the integrity and value of our degrees.

I mention this explicitly as I do not want to find myself in the position of having to expel a student for plagiarism. Please make sure this doesn't happen.

Again, the professors will be happy to explain what is OK, and what is not, so there is no reason to start guessing.

Learn about the university's rules on plagiarism and cheating.

Form a study group

Studying on a master's programme can be tough. There is a lot of work, the others often look more focused or organized, and they have all plans for the future. I strongly recommend you team up in study groups and help each other – with the course work and life in general. It's all part of networking and you will realize that the others also have doubts about their choice of specialization.
