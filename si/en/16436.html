As you already know, the Food Science and Technology programme structure (FST) has three specialisations, Dairy Science and Technology, Plant Based Foods and General profile. 
What I would like to elaborate on here are the differences between the specialisations and some suggestions on how to find your OWN way through the programme. 
First of all, when the University accepted you, it was into the FST-programme, and even though you selected a specialisation, you can change your mind about the specialisation, as long as you can transfer all courses and don't prolong your programme. In layman's terms, this means that it is (quite simple) to change from the two specialisations with many compulsory courses (Dairy, and Plant Based) into the General profile, because the compulsory courses are also accepted as restricted elective courses. On the other hand, it is comparably complicated to change from General profile to the other two specialisations, and if you want to do this, you have to make the decision within the first blocks of your study. 
The good news is that all three specialisations provide excellent learning outcome and job opportunities, so do not stress too much about which specialisation you follow. 
The big difference is your degree of freedom. Within Dairy Science and Technology, most of the courses are compulsory, which provides less degrees of freedom and e.g. makes studies abroad more complicated, but the upside is that you do not have to do a lot of soul searching about which courses are right for you. In contrast, the General profile, and to some extent Plant Based Foods provides a very open canvas where you can piece together your own individual patchwork of courses. 
Within Dairy Science and Technology, the actual courses has been quite consistent for the last years, but the rules for internship have changed a lot, for students admitted September 2024 or later. If you were admitted in sept 2024 or later, you will start on a 2-year program, and if you are able to secure an industrial internship, you will be transferred to a 2.5 year program, including the internship. If you do not secure an internship, you will just continue on the two-year programme. Therefore it is still very important that you choose the appropriate admission year, when you look at your programme structure.
Within Plant Based Foods, this is specialisation is still very new, so there are no changes to the curriculum. One big advantage in this specialization is the opportunity to go abroad for one semester, without having to rearrange your courses. 
In contrast to the two specialisations mentioned above, the General profile provides a very open canvas where you can piece together your own individual patchwork of courses. This provides excellent opportunities to string together an individual programme and even include a study abroad without too many obstacles. The downside of this freedom is that YOU must decide what is right for you and sometimes that can be a little intimidating. 
In the next section, I will show the recommended study tracks, which only apply to the General profile. I would like to finish this section with some considerations about elective courses. 
Elective courses are exactly what they say…elective, and that means that I cannot, and I will not suggest specific courses. You have either 15 or 30 elective courses dependent on your choice of thesis length (30 or 45 ECTS), which means that the university and I have already suggested/mandated 105 or 90 ECTS for you to follow.
Therefore, it does not make sense that I also come with suggestions for the remaining ECTS….it is YOUR study and YOUR decision, but I can give you some general thoughts. The competence profile of your education is determined by your compulsory and limited elective courses, so whatever you choose as elective, it is not reflected in your competence profile. A consequence of this is that you cannot select a “wrong" elective course. If you decide that you want to follow an elective course in advanced seismology, you have the right to do so. I am not sure why you would want to follow such a course, but it is beyond the point, because you can if you want. Of course, most of you will choose differently, but some of you may be interested in statistics, and others in nutrition and a third group in chemistry, and all these options are right if they are right for YOU. 
No matter which specialisation you choose, I would recommend that you look at my recommended study tracks for the general specialisation below, because you can find many interesting courses in these recommendations. 
And very important. To finish your programme, you must have passed all your courses, but the sequence may vary, so you can e.g. reverse an elective and a limited elective or follow a compulsory course at a different time of your study. Again, it is YOUR plan, and even though our suggestions are sensible, maybe another sequence will make more sense to you.
