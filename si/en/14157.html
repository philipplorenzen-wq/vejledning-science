If you have a functional impairment, it is a good idea to consider what this means for you in a job situation    and in a workplace. For your future employer, it is important that you are aware of what you are good at and what you are not so good at.

Use the strengths model to focus on what you are good at

The strengths model can help you understand and verbalise your strengths and skills. You will need that when presenting your qualifications in relation to a job and a workplace. 

 

Open the strenghts model as PDF

Your impairment may impact on some of the corners in the model, but probably not all of them. Often, you can do much more than you think. Here are a couple of examples:

If you are dyslexic, this may affect your skills – how you work, but not your personality, your knowledge or your motivation. You may need extra time to read things or prepare for something in writing – and it is important to inform a future employer about this.

If you have autism or a mental impairment, this may impact your personality and your skills. This could be that you are a bit quiet and may feel insecure if there are many people around you. On the other hand, you may be good at immersing yourself in complex tasks. Your impairment does probably not affect your knowledge or your motivation, which are just as important strengths.

Where does your functional impairment matter - and where not?

 
  Skills - What are you good at?

Skills can be your methods or approaches to work. For example, you work in a structured manner, you are good at collaboration (describe how), you have an understanding of processes or of large organisations.
 

 
  Personality - Who are you?

Personality is what characterises you. Your personal competences are, for example, when you are goal-oriented, responsible or good at keeping your cool.
 

 
  Motivation - What would you like to do?

Motivation is where you find energy in what you are doing. Are there particular topics that motivate you? Or certain working methods that make you more productive?
 

 
  Knowledge - What do you know?

Knowledge is what you have learned on the courses you have taken. Knowledge typically refers to everything you have learned about one or more topics through experience or in the classroom.
