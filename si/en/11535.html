One of the challenges that some students, who have been abroad, experience when returning home is reversed culture shock.

Just as with culture shock it differs from person to person how much or in what way you are affected.

When returning home many students may:

  • Expect to meet an idealised view of home.
  • Expect to be met with complete familiarity.


Problems some time occur when expectations are not met. Things back home will, to some extent, have changed while you were away (family, friends, circumstances, things have happened that you were not a part of, etc.).

You will, to some extent, have changed while away:

  • Might have reexamined your values and priorities.
  • Have been introduced to a different way of life.
  • Have been introduced to a different approach to your field of study.
  • Have a bag full of experiences that no one back home has taken part in.


These mixed expectations might leave you feeling:

  • That there is a lack of interest in your experiences from family and friends.
  • That you miss your new friends.
  • That you miss the experience you had abroad (country, way of life, values, feeling of independence, etc.).
  • An overall inconsistency between expectations and reality.


This might lead you to feel:

  • Alienated
  • Frustrated
  • Misunderstood


It is said that the more integrated you become while abroad the harder it can be to re-enter back home. If you experience symptoms of reversed culture shock make sure to talk to somebody. This could be:

  • A study abroad counselor or international coordinator at your home university.
  • One of your new friends, who might be going through some of the same experiences.


Know that it is completely normal, if you feel symptoms of reversed culture shock - and that things will get easier with time and you will re-adjust.

Make sure to integrate the positive aspects of your study abroad experience into your life back home. This will also help the process.
