How can we structure our meetings in the study group?

A good meeting structure can help you to hold effective, fruitful study group meetings, where you manage to do what you were planning to do. 


Below are 4 simple tools for better meetings:

1) Manage your time and stick to the agreed topics 

It is a good idea to make an agenda before your meeting, so you: 

  • know how to prepare for the meeting 
  • cover all items at the meeting. 

You could make it a fixed routine to create an agenda for the next meeting before the end of each study group meeting.

AGENDA TEMPLATE


2) Keep minutes, so you remember what you have decided 

Minutes are a written summary of your meeting. The minutes basically outline what you have done and what you have decided. Decide in the group how detailed the minutes should be. 

The minutes are useful if: 

  • you come up with some good ideas or make some decisions that you need to remember 
  • a group member is sick and wants to know what you discussed. 

You could enter the minutes directly into your agenda. 


3) Kickstart the discussions 

3 ways to get people talking at your meetings, that take different personality types into account: 

  • Chat in pairs: Discuss in pairs what you find difficult/fun/interesting about the assignment, text or topic. 
  • Silent reflection: As a warm-up for the group discussion, you can each sit quietly for 5 minutes and reflect on what you have read/the assignments you have completed. 
  • The round: Each member speaks for 2 minutes about what they found difficult/fun/interesting.

4) Improve your concentration 

Research shows that fresh air, physical activity and variation enhance your concentration.


4 things you can do to improve concentration: 

  • Go for a walk around the block or in a nearby park. 
  • Hold your study group meeting in a new location occasionally, e.g. the home of one of the group members, the university or a café. 
  • Do star jumps or squat jumps for two minutes. 
  • Air the room. 
  • Stand up during the group work.
