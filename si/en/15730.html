The MSc Landscape Architecture programme trains students in critical analysis, planning and design of built environments and landscapes with an emphasis on sustainability, democracy, and quality of life for more than humans. The central approach to urban design and landscape architecture are planning and design methods. Therefore, we make site-specific and solution-oriented planning and design proposals for all kinds of problems in urban space and in the open countryside, such as adaptation to climate change and loss of biodiversity.

Solution-oriented project work shapes your academic approach and develops your design skills and competencies. Human curiosity and academic wondering are expected in each course, while landscape architectural design and planning aspirations guide both site analysis, sketching and research-through-design.

Good scientific conduct also applies to landscape architecture. In addition to literature and scientific sources, reference to design precedents and reference photos are also part of the rules of good scientific conduct.

Many courses are project-driven and focus on a specific subject in planning and design. This will give you a variety of perspectives and methodical approaches and deepen your planning and design skills. The subject-specific work is intended to provide specialised knowledge, skills and competences and the same time to strengthen your general academic competences and your individual landscape architectural design experience.In the international study environment, we expect everyone to participate in group work despite cultural differences and to involve supervisors when group work is not working. We expect you to notify fellow students if you are running late etc. We also expect you to work individually for the group if for any reason you are unable to attend scheduled group work sessions. All group work begins with the exchange of expectations among each other.
Throughout the study programme, you focus on professional practice. Project work relates to real places and authentic societal problems. Many courses are carried out in cooperation with external practice partners, for example a municipality, and the students develop and test their design ideas in dialogue with municipal planners and local actors. Professional colleagues act as guest lecturers, teaching assistants and external examiners. In addition, a large part of the research of the teachers also relates to landscape architecture and planning practice. 

Project in Practice (PIP) is a specific study element aimed at professional practice experience. It is therefore important that the students actively participate in practice. Carrying out the PIP over a longer period in combination with the summer break, e.g., in Block 4 or Block 1 is recommended. PIP can also be combined with the thesis.

The study week is separated into three schedule groups (A, B, C). The course activities in a 15 ECTS course cover two groups and the third is for homework, reading and preparation. However, each individual student is free to schedule their own time, as long as the student participates in course activities and completes homework/group work according to a 12-hour workload.

The four blocks of 9 weeks each are the main study period. But the week between the blocks can also be used for conferences, re-exam etc.Guest lectures are offered throughout the year – many in the form of  “Copenhagen Landscape Lectures" or as public guest lectures in conjunction with specific courses.
