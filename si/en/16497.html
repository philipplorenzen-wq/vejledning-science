The Danish labour market is known for:

  • a high amount of flexibility and job mobility
  • good working conditions and a secure support system.

The labour marked conditions are primarily regulated by different actors within the labour marked itself in contrast to being regulated by legislations. For example there is no legislation regarding minimum wages, and pay and working conditions are typically set by collective agreements negotiated by trade unions and employers' organization.

Danish legislation does, however, include some minimum requirements within areas as holiday, parental leave, illness, equal treatment etc.

This way of structuring the labour marked is often referred to as the Danish labour market model.

Read more about the Danish labour market model on work in Denmark.dk 

Unemployment insurance funds and unions

For you as a student - and later as an employee - there are 2 types of organisations, that

  • represent your rights on the labour market
  • negotiate your working conditions
  • support you with unemployment funds
  • guides you in your student and working life.

We recommend you to consider becoming a member of both a union (fagforening) and an unemployment insurance fund (a-kasse) already while you are studying, because they have relevant offers for you, like access to career guidance, events and a professional network.

You may be able to become a member of an a-kasse and receive unemployment funds after you graduate. However, different rules apply depending on your citizenship and visa status.
